Halter Ranch Conservation Scholars (HARCS) — Cal Poly

The Halter Ranch Conservation Scholars (HARCS) program provides hands-on learning opportunities and financial support for Cal Poly undergraduates pursuing careers in conservation. The program combines a scholarship, a 10-week spring seminar, and a paid summer internship at a partner conservation organization — giving students a continuous learn-then-apply pathway from the classroom to an active conservation project.



Who the program is for

HARCS is designed for Cal Poly undergraduates with a genuine interest in applied conservation, regardless of major. Successful scholars typically:

  • Are curious about California’s flora, fauna, and working landscapes.
  • Are comfortable spending full days outdoors in variable terrain and weather.
  • Are motivated to design, carry out, and communicate an independent project.

No prior research experience is required. Scholars come from biology, environmental management, animal science, agricultural science, and other related programs.

What scholars do

Spring seminar. Weekly in-classroom meetings paired with field experiences at reserves and natural areas across San Luis Obispo County. The course culminates in a weekend field trip to a preserve and in final presentations of each scholar’s independent project proposal.

Summer internship. After the seminar, each scholar transitions to a paid summer internship at a partner conservation organization. The internship is where the scholar’s spring proposal becomes a real project: collecting data, contributing to ongoing monitoring, building maps, and producing deliverables the host organization can use.

Current projects

Rangeland assessments and Beaver Dam Analog site selection at Rana Creek Preserve

Interns: Finn Torres & Natalie Donnelson — Rana Creek Preserve, The Wildlands Conservancy

Finn and Natalie are working on two major projects at the Rana Creek Preserve, guided by the preserve manager, Kat Hardisty-Cranstone. The first is a rangeland monitoring program that the interns helped to develop and are implementing for the first time this year. The preserve is actively grazed, and the cows were fitted with collars this year to allow for virtual fencing. This allows for greater adaptability to control where and at what intensity the cows are grazing. As this project continues, the monitoring data can be used to determine what grazing strategies are having a positive impact on rangeland health.

The second major project is surveying the riparian areas on the preserve to identify potential areas for installing beaver dam analogs (BDA’s) and eventual beaver introductions. BDA’s are human made structures that mimic beaver dams and help restore creeks and streams to a healthy condition. By identifying suitable areas and installing BDA’s, the preserve will raise groundwater levels, decrease erosion, and create habitat for fish, amphibians, riparian vegetation and eventually, beavers.

Beaver Dam Analog installation and monitoring with SRWC

Intern: Landon Miller — Scott River Watershed Council

Landon is working with the Scott River Watershed Council on installing BDA’s in the Scott River Watershed, located in the Klamath Mountains of Northern California. The council is focusing on installing BDAs in degraded mountain meadows, where the structures will help reduce channel incision and raise the groundwater level, restoring the natural hydrology that makes these meadows home to a huge diversity of native grasses and wildflowers. Landon has placed wildlife cameras at BDA sites, to record wildlife use of the sites before and after the structures are installed. This will help inform how the creation of ponds affects even animals that don’t directly live in them.

Cascades Frogs habitat assessment with the SRWC and Cal Fish and Wildlife

Intern: Rowan Campbell — Scott River Watershed Council & CDFW

Rowan is working with SRWC and CDFW to better understand how the Cascades Frog (a candidate species for endangered status) uses mountain meadows. In addition to surveying meadows to assess their ecological health with SRWC, he is collaborating with CDFW to survey meadows for Cascades Frogs. Healthy meadows with intact hydrology are more likely to host shallow ponds that are breeding habitat for Cascades Frogs. Rowan’s work may eventually link restoring the hydrology of meadows using tools like BDAs to improved breeding habitat for Cascades Frogs.

Post-fire vegetation management at Bear Paw Preserve

Interns: Rylyn Jaggar & Rylie Helberg — Bear Paw Preserve, The Wildlands Conservancy

Rylyn and Rylie at Bear Paw Preserve are focused on understanding how vegetation management on the preserve impacts wildlife. Bear Paw Preserve was completely burned at high severity in the 2020 El Dorado Fire. The preserve, previously composed of mature Black Oak and Big Cone Douglas Fir woodland, is now dominated by vigorously resprouting shrub species such as Ceanothus and Choke Cherry. Under natural fire regimes, forests would have burned with mixed severity and trees would have only been fully killed in some areas, creating a patchwork of trees and shrubs. While part of the natural regeneration process, when they are too widespread, shrubs can prevent tree seedlings from re-establishing and increase the probability that an area will burn again. The staff at Bear Paw have been able to remove the shrubs in some areas, in an attempt to reduce fire hazard and restore oak trees to the area. Rylyn and Rylie are monitoring a network of wildlife cameras spread throughout the treated and untreated areas, conducting plant surveys, recording bird activity, and measuring soil chemistry, all to assess the impacts and benefits of the vegetation management on the preserve.

Land Conservation in San Luis Obispo County

Intern: Santiago Olvera — Land Conservancy of San Luis Obispo County

Santiago is working locally with the Land Conservancy of San Luis Obispo County. He is focused on learning about the process of establishing conservation easements, through helping with baseline documentation of a new easement. He is also shadowing teams working on ecosystem restoration, ecological monitoring, and community outreach.
